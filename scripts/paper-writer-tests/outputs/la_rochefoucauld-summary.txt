In my reflections on human nature, I have come to a central understanding: human behavior is fundamentally driven by self-love and illusion. This insight forms the core thesis of my philosophical work. By self-love, I refer to an innate affection for oneself that influences our actions and perceptions. Illusion, on the other hand, is the false perception or belief that often accompanies our self-love, distorting reality to suit our desires and self-image.

One of the most telling manifestations of this self-love is our tendency to rewrite our past. We are not merely passive observers of our history; rather, we actively reshape it to enhance our self-image. This rewriting is not a conscious deception but an unconscious process driven by our desire to see ourselves in a favorable light. We selectively recall events, emphasizing our successes and downplaying our failures, sometimes attributing them to external circumstances beyond our control. This selective reconstruction serves to bolster our self-esteem and justify our actions.

Another compelling aspect of human nature is the effectiveness of flattery. Flattery works because it appeals directly to our innate self-love. We are predisposed to believe the best about ourselves, and when others affirm this belief, it strengthens our self-image. Flattery, therefore, is not merely a social nicety but a powerful tool that exploits our inherent tendency towards self-deception. It is a reflection of our desire to be seen as we wish to be, rather than as we are.

The implications of these insights into human nature are profound. They challenge the notion of pure altruism, suggesting instead that our actions, even those seemingly selfless, are often motivated by self-interest. We are driven by a desire to maintain a positive self-image, and this desire shapes our interactions with others. It leads us to act in ways that align with our self-perception, often at the expense of objective truth.
