The implications of this argument are profound. If we accept that legitimate authority arises from the social contract, then we must reject the notion that force creates right. Power, when divorced from the consent of the governed, becomes tyranny. Each must vote not as a private individual pursuing gain, but as a member of the sovereign body considering the welfare of all. The sovereign - that collective body of all citizens - cannot err when it truly expresses the general will. I have observed these corruptions in Geneva, my beloved but troubled city. There, patrician families monopolized power while claiming to represent the people. They spoke of liberty while denying citizens their rightful voice. Such hypocrisy reveals how easily the social contract can be perverted.

The remedy lies not in abandoning the contract but in purifying it. Citizens must be educated to distinguish between their role as private individuals and their duty as members of the sovereign. They must learn to silence their particular interests when the common good demands it.

This education cannot be left to chance. A republic requires citizens of virtue - men who prize the public good above personal advantage. Without such virtue, the social contract becomes mere words on parchment. The general will degenerates into the will of the strongest faction.

I do not propose that men become angels. Human nature remains what it is - a mixture of self-interest and moral capacity. But institutions can channel these impulses toward beneficial ends. When citizens participate directly in lawmaking, when they feel genuine ownership of their government, they develop the civic virtue necessary for freedom.

The alternative is the chains I have described - not merely political oppression, but the deeper bondage of moral corruption. When men cease to care for anything beyond their private concerns, they become slaves to their appetites. They lose the very capacity for freedom that makes them human.

The corruption spreads through education itself. We teach children to compete rather than cooperate, to prize individual success over collective flourishing. From their earliest years, we prepare them for a society of mutual antagonism rather than genuine association. Is it any wonder that such citizens cannot sustain free institutions?

True education must begin with nature. The child must learn to feel before he learns to think, to love his fellow creatures before he calculates his advantage over them. Only then can he understand that his freedom depends entirely on respecting the freedom of others.

This is not mere sentiment. It is political necessity. A government of laws requires citizens who internalize those laws as expressions of their own will. When men obey only from fear or habit, they remain essentially enslaved. Their compliance masks a fundamental rejection of the social bond.

I have been accused of romanticism, of imagining men better than they are. My critics mistake my purpose. The social contract is not a description but a prescription - not what is, but what ought to be.

Yet the prescription remains realistic. Men need not become perfect to sustain free government. This recognition comes not from abstract reasoning but from lived experience of genuine participation.

When citizens gather in assembly to deliberate on common concerns, when they feel the weight of collective decision-making, they develop what I call the civic sense. They begin to think in terms of "we" rather than merely "I." This transformation is both psychological and political - a rebirth of human nature itself.

The social contract thus represents more than a theory of government. It offers a path toward human redemption, a way of recovering the natural goodness that civilization has corrupted.
