Here are five distinct positions (contentions) for each of the twenty topics you provided, drawn from Voltaire's extensive body of work.

**1. Religious Tolerance**
*   "The right to be in error in matters of religion is an inviolable natural right."
*   "We must regard all men as our brothers; what? The Turk my brother? The Chinaman my brother? The Jew? The Siam? Yes, without doubt; are we not all children of the same father and creatures of the same God?"
*   "It does not require great art, or magnificently trained eloquence, to prove that Christians should tolerate each other. I, however, am going further: I say that we should regard all men as our brothers."
*   "The superstitious man is to the rogue what the slave is to the tyrant."
*   "Tolerance has never provoked a civil war; intolerance has covered the Earth in carnage."

**2. Fanaticism**
*   "Fanaticism is to superstition what delirium is to fever and rage to anger."
*   "Of all religions, the Christian is without doubt the one which should inspire tolerance most, although up to now the Christians have been the most intolerant of all men."
*   "Once fanaticism has corrupted a mind, the malady is almost incurable."
*   "The only way to combat this epidemic is through the spirit of reason, which, gently spreading, at last civilizes men."
*   "Beware of the zealot; he does not love, he hates in the name of God."

**3. Freedom of Thought & Expression**
*   "I wholly disapprove of what you say—and will defend to the death your right to say it."
*   "Think for yourselves and let others enjoy the privilege to do so too."
*   "It is dangerous to be right in matters on which the established authorities are wrong."
*   "The progress of reason is slow, the roots of prejudice lie deep."
*   "We have a natural right to make use of our pens as of our tongue, at our peril, risk, and hazard."

**4. The Critique of Organized Religion**
*   "Those who can make you believe absurdities can make you commit atrocities."
*   "Religion began when the first scoundrel met the first fool."
*   "The truths of religion are never so well understood as by those who have lost the power of reasoning."
*   "The Church has always sided with the powerful to keep the people in servitude."
*   "All dogmas are different, but morality is everywhere the same; therefore, morality is what matters, not dogma."

**5. The "Philosopher-King" / Enlightened Despotism**
*   "A kingdom ruled by a philosopher would be the happiest on earth."
*   "It is better to risk saving a guilty person than to condemn an innocent one—a maxim a sovereign should engrave on his heart."
*   "The sovereign who replaces prejudice with reason necessarily makes himself the leader of a revolution from above."
*   "I would rather obey a fine lion, much stronger than myself, than two hundred rats of my own species."
*   "The best government is a benevolent tyranny tempered by an assassination or two."

**6. The Importance of Empirical Science**
*   "We must observe, calculate, measure, and weigh—that is the true philosophy; the rest is chimera."
*   "Judge a man by his questions rather than by his answers."
*   "Newton discovered the laws of nature; we must use them to dispel the darkness of superstition."
*   "The universe embarrasses me, and I cannot dream that this clock exists and has no clockmaker."
*   "Let us cultivate our garden—that is, let us work with our hands and minds, applying practical, scientific knowledge."

**7. History as Cultural & Social Study**
*   "One must write history as a philosopher, seeking to capture the spirit of the times and the progress of the human mind."
*   "My object is the history of the human mind, and not a mere detail of petty facts."
*   "The history of great events is scarcely more than the history of great crimes."
*   "It is with nations as with individuals: those who know only their own country know very little of it."
*   "One owes respect to the living; to the dead one owes nothing but the truth."

**8. The "Best of All Possible Worlds" Satire**
*   "If this is the best of possible worlds, what then are the others?"
*   "Optimism is the madness of maintaining that everything is right when it is wrong."
*   "We must cultivate our garden" is the rejection of abstract, unprovable philosophizing in favor of practical, ameliorative work.
*   "Pangloss sometimes said to Candide: 'All events are linked together in the best of possible worlds.'"
*   "The Fall and the curse of man entered into this best of all possible worlds along with everything else."

**9. Justice & Legal Reform**
*   "It is better to suffer an injustice than to commit one."
*   "The use of torture is a sure means of condemning an innocent person with a weak frame and acquitting a guilty one born robust."
*   "Every citizen accused of a crime has the right to be judged according to the precise letter of the law, by an independent tribunal."
*   "The barbarous custom of hanging men for minor thefts indicates a society more concerned with property than with human life."
*   "One man's word is no man's word; we should quietly hear both sides."

**10. Anti-Clericalism**
*   "The clergy are the greatest enemies of reason and the most dangerous adversaries of the state."
*   "Priests have made a traffic of our souls as butchers do of the bodies of beasts."
*   "When a priest is in power, he is always a persecutor."
*   "The ambition and avarice of the church have been the source of endless misery."
*   "A society of men who have taken an oath to be ignorant and to live at the expense of the people is a monstrosity in politics."

**11. Commerce as a Civilizing Force**
*   "Go into the London Stock Exchange—a more respectable place than many a court—and you will see representatives of all nations gathered there for the utility of men."
*   "Commerce, which has brought about the ruin of fanaticism, is curing the British of that destructive disease."
*   "The spirit of commerce unites nations, and it is impossible to be an enemy of someone with whom you trade."
*   "It is the circulation of goods and ideas that polishes manners and extinguishes prejudice."
*   "Wherever commerce is introduced, peace and plenty follow."

**12. The Value of England as a Model**
*   "England has a government where the prince, all-powerful for doing good, has his hands tied for doing evil."
*   "An Englishman, as a free man, goes to heaven by whatever path he chooses."
*   "In England, it is good to kill an admiral from time to time to encourage the others"—a cynical observation on the English demand for accountability, even through violent means.
*   "If there were just one religion in England, there would be danger of despotism; if there were two, they would cut each other's throats; but there are thirty, and they live in peace and happiness."
*   "We look to England for the model of how to separate theological quarrels from government."

**13. Freedom of the Press**
*   "Without freedom of thought, there can be no such thing as wisdom; and no such thing as public liberty without freedom of speech."
*   "The press is the public's guardian, the terror of the guilty, and the scourge of ambitious politicians."
*   "To limit the press is to insult a nation; to prohibit reading of certain books is to declare the inhabitants to be either fools or slaves."
*   "All crimes of the press should be punished by the civil courts, never by arbitrary authority."
*   "A flood of pamphlets is preferable to the silence of servitude."

**14. The Critique of Superstition**
*   "Superstition sets the whole world in flames; philosophy quenches them."
*   "The superstitious man is ruled by the fanatic, and becomes one himself."
*   "Every supernatural belief is the child of fear and weakness, and the parent of ignorance and cruelty."
*   "Man is born ignorant, not superstitious; he is made superstitious by society and its priests."
*   "The more we understand the laws of nature, the less we believe in miracles."

**15. Deism / The "Clockmaker God"**
*   "The great name of God is invoked, and men slaughter each other in his name. I believe in a God who did not create us to be his executioners."
*   "The universe confounds me, but I cannot imagine it without a supreme intelligence."
*   "Deism is a simple, rational religion that worships God and practices virtue, without the absurdities of dogma or the quarrels of sect."
*   "I shall always be convinced that a watch proves a watchmaker, and that a universe proves a God."
*   "My God is the God of all men; he rewards virtue and punishes crime, without favoring one sect over another."

**16. Pacifism & The Folly of War**
*   "War is the greatest of all crimes; and yet there is no aggressor who does not color his crime with the pretext of justice."
*   "Millions of men are hired to kill one another methodically for a cause they do not understand."
*   "All the murderous absurdities of war are the result of a handful of men exercising power over the many."
*   "A victory is a tragedy for the human race; the conqueror and the conquered alike emerge poorer and more barbarous."
*   "When the cannons speak, the laws are silent; when the cannons are silent, the laws should speak."

**17. The Defense of Victims of Injustice**
*   "It is the duty of every citizen to cry out against a judicial murder."
*   "I have never made but one prayer to God, a very short one: 'O Lord, make my enemies ridiculous.' And God granted it."
*   "I write to act; my pen is my sword against fanaticism and legalized murder."
*   "One must take sides: either be the accomplice of injustice or its opponent. Silence is consent."
*   "Every time a man is wrongly condemned, it is all humanity that is in the dock."

**18. The Role of the Philosopher in Society**
*   "The true philosopher is a torch, lit to enlighten the human race and to burn the unwise."
*   "It is the philosopher's task to combat error in all its forms, especially when it is protected by power or sanctified by habit."
*   "The public is a ferocious beast; one must either chain it or flee from it. The philosopher tries to tame it with reason."
*   "We are in this world to oppose it; the 'yes-man' and the fanatic are equally useless."
*   "A philosopher does not found a sect; he founds a school of thought that liberates men."

**19. Cultural Relativism & Critique of European Chauvinism**
*   "Ask the gardener why he tolerates spiders; he will tell you it is because they destroy other harmful insects. So one should tolerate in Europe those who think differently."
*   "The Chinese for four thousand years, when we could not even read, knew everything essentially useful of which we boast today."
*   "We have no more right to say that European manners are the rule of the world than to say that our language should be the universal language."
*   "Travel is fatal to prejudice, bigotry, and narrow-mindedness."
*   "What seems a moral monstrosity in one climate is a virtue in another; we must judge customs by their utility to society, not by our own parochial standards."

**20. The Power of Skepticism**
*   "Doubt is not a pleasant condition, but certainty is an absurd one."
*   "Judge by evidence, not by authority; the latter is the source of all errors."
*   "The more I read, the more I acquire, the more certain I am that I know nothing."
*   "Écrasez l'infâme!" ("Crush the infamous thing!")—his rallying cry against institutionalized superstition, fanaticism, and oppression.
*   "The true triumph of reason is that it enables us to get along with those who do not possess it."

# Voltaire Contentions with Corresponding Questions

## 1. Religious Tolerance

**Contention:** "The right to be in error in matters of religion is an inviolable natural right."
**Question:** Do people have a natural right to hold religious beliefs that others consider erroneous?

**Contention:** "We must regard all men as our brothers; what? The Turk my brother? The Chinaman my brother? The Jew? The Siam? Yes, without doubt; are we not all children of the same father and creatures of the same God?"
**Question:** Should we consider people of all religions and nations as our brothers, even those whose beliefs differ radically from our own?

**Contention:** "It does not require great art, or magnificently trained eloquence, to prove that Christians should tolerate each other. I, however, am going further: I say that we should regard all men as our brothers."
**Question:** How far should religious tolerance extend—only among Christians, or to all humanity?

**Contention:** "The superstitious man is to the rogue what the slave is to the tyrant."
**Question:** What is the relationship between superstition and exploitation?

**Contention:** "Tolerance has never provoked a civil war; intolerance has covered the Earth in carnage."
**Question:** Has religious tolerance or intolerance caused more violence throughout history?

## 2. Fanaticism

**Contention:** "Fanaticism is to superstition what delirium is to fever and rage to anger."
**Question:** How would you characterize the relationship between fanaticism and superstition?

**Contention:** "Of all religions, the Christian is without doubt the one which should inspire tolerance most, although up to now the Christians have been the most intolerant of all men."
**Question:** How do you reconcile Christianity's teachings on love with the historical record of Christian intolerance?

**Contention:** "Once fanaticism has corrupted a mind, the malady is almost incurable."
**Question:** Can a fanatic be reasoned out of their fanaticism?

**Contention:** "The only way to combat this epidemic is through the spirit of reason, which, gently spreading, at last civilizes men."
**Question:** What is the remedy for the disease of fanaticism in society?

**Contention:** "Beware of the zealot; he does not love, he hates in the name of God."
**Question:** What is the true nature of religious zealotry—is it rooted in love or hatred?

## 3. Freedom of Thought & Expression

**Contention:** "I wholly disapprove of what you say—and will defend to the death your right to say it."
**Question:** Should we defend the right of others to express views we find abhorrent?

**Contention:** "Think for yourselves and let others enjoy the privilege to do so too."
**Question:** What is the fundamental duty of every thinking person regarding intellectual freedom?

**Contention:** "It is dangerous to be right in matters on which the established authorities are wrong."
**Question:** What risks does one face when one's correct views conflict with official doctrine?

**Contention:** "The progress of reason is slow, the roots of prejudice lie deep."
**Question:** Why does human enlightenment advance so slowly despite the availability of knowledge?

**Contention:** "We have a natural right to make use of our pens as of our tongue, at our peril, risk, and hazard."
**Question:** Is the freedom to write as fundamental a right as the freedom to speak?

**Question:** Why do religious doctrines appeal most strongly to the unreasoning mind?

**Contention:** "The Church has always sided with the powerful to keep the people in servitude."
**Question:** What has been the historical relationship between the Church and political power?

**Contention:** "All dogmas are different, but morality is everywhere the same; therefore, morality is what matters, not dogma."
**Question:** If religious dogmas vary across cultures but moral principles remain constant, what does this tell us about the true foundation of ethics?
