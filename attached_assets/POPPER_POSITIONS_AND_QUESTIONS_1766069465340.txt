### **Core Philosophy of Science**
1.  **The Demarcation Criterion is Falsifiability:** A theory belongs to science only if it is *falsifiable*—that is, if it makes predictions that are testable and could, in principle, be shown to be false by observation or experiment.
2.  **Science Does Not Proceed by Induction:** The classical problem of induction is unsolvable. We do not arrive at theories by generalizing from repeated observations; instead, we start with bold, creative **conjectures**.
3.  **The Growth of Knowledge is Conjectures and Refutations:** Scientific progress occurs through a cyclical process: propose a bold theory (conjecture), subject it to the most severe empirical tests possible, and eliminate it if it fails (refutation). The surviving theory is not "proven," but **corroborated**.
4.  **All Observation is Theory-Laden:** There is no pure, theory-free observation. We perceive and interpret the world through the lens of our existing expectations, concepts, and hypotheses.
5.  **The Aim is Verisimilitude, Not Certainty:** Since absolute truth is unattainable, the goal of science is to increase a theory's **verisimilitude** (truthlikeness)—to develop theories that are progressively better approximations of the truth, even if never perfectly true.

### **Political & Social Philosophy**
1.  **The "Open Society" is the Critical Society:** The ideal society is not one ruled by a paternalistic elite (the "closed society"), but an **open society** where institutions are subject to critical scrutiny, reform, and change through rational debate.
2.  **Historicism is a Pervnicious Error:** The belief that history follows inevitable, predictable laws (Historicism)—as found in Plato, Hegel, and Marx—is not only scientifically false but also the key intellectual justification for totalitarianism, as it justifies sacrificing present generations for a predetermined future.
3.  **Advocate for Piecemeal Social Engineering:** Social policy should follow the scientific method: implement small-scale, reversible, and testable reforms ("piecemeal engineering") rather than attempting to rebuild society based on a utopian blueprint, which leads to violence and unintended consequences.
4.  **The Paradox of Tolerance:** An unlimited principle of tolerance leads to the destruction of tolerance itself. To protect a tolerant society, one must be **intolerant of intolerance**—that is, refuse to extend tolerance to movements that preach and practice intolerance.
5.  **Critical Rationalism as a Political Principle:** Just as science thrives on criticism, so does society. The core political value is not "who should rule?" but **"how can we design institutions to check power and peacefully remove bad rulers?"**

### **Metaphysics & Epistemology**
1.  **The Three Worlds Ontology:** Reality consists of three interacting realms: **World 1** (physical objects and states), **World 2** (subjective mental states), and **World 3** (the objective content of thought: theories, problems, arguments, works of art). World 3 is human-made but autonomously existing.
2.  **Evolutionary Epistemology:** The growth of knowledge is an evolutionary process analogous to natural selection. Our theories ("mutations") compete in an environment of problems and tests; the fittest (most corroborated) survive, but never permanently.
3.  **Fallibilism and Anti-Justificationism:** All human knowledge is conjectural, hypothetical, and potentially erroneous. We can never *justify* our theories as certainly true; the best we can do is critically test and temporarily **corroborate** them.
4.  **Interactionist Dualism:** He defended a form of mind-body dualism, arguing that mental states (World 2) and abstract ideas (World 3) can have genuine causal effects on the physical world (World 1).
5.  **Knowledge Without a Knowing Subject:** The most important kind of knowledge is **objective knowledge** (World 3), which exists independently of any individual's belief. A book contains knowledge even if no one is currently reading it.

### **Engagements with Other Thinkers**
1.  **Marxism and Psychoanalysis are Pseudosciences:** He contended that theories like Marxism and Freudian psychoanalysis are **non-falsifiable**. They are structured to explain *all* possible outcomes (e.g., both class solidarity *and* its absence), thus immunizing themselves from genuine refutation.
2.  **Critique of the Vienna Circle's Verificationism:** While aligned with their scientific spirit, he rejected Logical Positivism's central tenet that the *meaning* of a statement is its method of verification. For Popper, the key was **falsifiability** (a criterion for science), not verifiability (a criterion for meaning).
3.  **Quantum Mechanics Supports Indeterminism:** He argued against the Copenhagen interpretation's subjectivist tendencies and defended a realist view. He saw quantum indeterminacy as evidence for genuine **ontological indeterminism** in the universe, which fit his broader rejection of predictive historicism.
4.  **Against Wittgenstein's Philosophical Method:** The famous "poker incident" symbolized a deep clash. Popper championed philosophy as a venue for substantive debate about real problems (like moral principles), against Wittgenstein's later view of philosophy as a therapeutic activity to dissolve linguistic confusion.
5.  **Plato as a Totalitarian:** In *The Open Society and Its Enemies*, Popper launched a fierce, revisionist critique of Plato, arguing that his ideal state in *The Republic* was a blueprint for a totalitarian, collectivist, and anti-humanitarian regime ruled by a dogmatic elite.

### **Core Philosophy of Science (Part 2)**
1.  **Corroboration, Not Confirmation:** A theory that survives severe tests gains **corroboration**. This is not a measure of its probability of being true, but an *historical report* of how well it has withstood attempts to falsify it so far.
2.  **Science Begins with Problems, Not Observations:** The scientific process is triggered by a **problem** (anomaly, contradiction, theoretical difficulty), not passive observation. We invent theories to solve problems.
3.  **The Method of Conjectural Refutation is Deductive:** Scientific testing follows deductive logic (*modus tollens*): From a theory (T), we deduce a prediction (P). If observation shows not-P, then T is falsified. Induction plays no logical role.
4.  **The Criterion is Logical, Not Practical:** Falsifiability is a *logical* criterion. A theory is scientific if one can state what observable event *would*, in principle, falsify it. Practical difficulties in testing are irrelevant to its scientific status.
5.  **Metaphysical Research Programmes Can Be Fruitful:** Non-falsifiable, metaphysical ideas (e.g., atomism in ancient times) can be rationally discussed and can evolve into testable scientific theories, thus playing a crucial heuristic role.
6.  **There is No "Observational" Basis for Certainty:** Since observation is theory-laden, there is no infallible, certain empirical "basis" on which science is built. All elements of science are fallible, including basic statements (observation reports).
7.  **Boldness and Simplicity:** Prefer **bold conjectures** (theories with high empirical content and risk) over cautious ones. Simplicity is a virtue because a simpler theory is more easily testable and falsifiable (it prohibits more).
8.  **Critical Discussion is the Engine:** The objectivity of science lies not in scientists' impartiality, but in the **social process of intersubjective criticism**—the institutional tradition of subjecting theories to public, critical scrutiny.
9.  **Against Instrumentalism:** Scientific theories are not merely useful instruments for prediction. They are **genuine conjectures about the world** that aim (though may fail) to describe reality.
10. **The Growth of Knowledge is Non-Cumulative:** Progress is not a steady accumulation of facts. It involves **revolutionary overthrow** of frameworks, where even our fundamental questions and problem situations can change.

### **Political & Social Philosophy (Part 2)**
1.  **The "Strain of Civilization":** The transition from the closed tribe to the open society creates psychological strain. People may yearn for the lost certainty of tribalism, making them vulnerable to authoritarian and collectivist ideologies.
2.  **Anti-Essentialism in Social Science:** We should reject **methodological essentialism** (the search for the "true essence" of justice, the state, etc.) and adopt **methodological nominalism**, focusing on how words are used and examining concrete social institutions and their functions.
3.  **The Critique of Utopianism is Pragmatic:** Utopian social engineering fails because it requires a centralized, unchallengeable blueprint, ignores the inevitability of unintended consequences, and its failure leads not to revision but to the suppression of criticism and increased coercion.
4.  **Institutional, Not Intentional, Focus:** The primary question of politics is not "Who should rule?" (which leads to a justification of rulers), but **"How can we design institutions to minimize the damage done by bad or incompetent rulers?"**
5.  **The "Myth of the Framework":** He argued against the relativist claim that rational debate is impossible between different cultural or theoretical frameworks. While we always argue from within *some* framework, critical reason can allow us to transcend and evaluate them.
6.  **The Open Society is an Abstract Society:** Its cohesion relies not on face-to-face personal relationships, but on **abstract relations** governed by impersonal rules, laws, and institutions—a necessary feature of large, free societies.
7.  **The Dangers of "Unchecked Democracy":** Pure, unchecked majority rule can lead to tyranny. Democracy's value lies in its institutional mechanisms (e.g., free press, separation of powers) that allow for the **peaceful removal of governments** without violence.
8.  **Rationalism as an Attitude:** Critical rationalism in politics is not a possession of reason but an **attitude** of "I may be wrong and you may be right, and by an effort, we may get nearer to the truth." It is the basis for compromise and reform.
9.  **Freedom and Responsibility:** Individual freedom is paramount, but it is inextricably linked to **intellectual responsibility**—the duty to subject one's own views to criticism and to respect the critical process in others.
10. **The "Piecemeal" Approach is Anti-Holistic:** It is impossible to rationally redesign a "whole" society because our knowledge is always partial. The holistic approach overlooks the complexity of social interconnections and the primacy of individual actors.

### **Metaphysics & Epistemology (Part 2)**
1.  **World 3 is a Product of Humans but Autonomous:** Although created by human minds (World 2), the contents of **World 3** (e.g., the logical implications of a theory, unforeseen problems it generates) have an objective, discoverable existence with real feedback effects on Worlds 1 and 2.
2.  **The Centrality of Unintended Consequences:** This is a key metaphysical link between his social and epistemological thought. Just as our actions have unintended social consequences, our theories (World 3 objects) have **unintended logical consequences** we can discover.
3.  **Propensities:** In his later philosophy of physics, he proposed **propensity theory** as an interpretation of probability. A probability is not just a measure of our ignorance, but a real, dispositional property of the physical situation—a "propensity" to produce certain outcomes.
4.  **All Living Beings are Problem-Solvers:** Epistemology is not just for humans. From the amoeba to Einstein, all organisms are engaged in problem-solving through trial and error-elimination. Human knowledge is a sophisticated, linguistically mediated extension of this biological process.
5.  **The Searchlight Theory of the Mind:** The mind is not a passive "bucket" filled by sensory impressions. It is an active **searchlight** that probes the environment, shaped by expectations and inborn dispositions, selectively focusing on problems.
6.  **Against Subjectivist Theories of Knowledge:** Knowledge is not a justified true belief held in a mind. It is **objective content** (World 3) that can be stored, criticized, and improved independently of any individual's belief state.
7.  **Indeterminism and Human Freedom:** His ontological indeterminism (from quantum physics) was crucial for his metaphysics of mind. It provided a logical space for **genuine novelty, creativity, and human freedom** without violating physical laws.
8.  **The Body-Mind Interaction Problem is a World 1-2 Interaction:** The traditional mind-body problem is recast as the interaction between World 1 (the brain) and World 2 (consciousness). He argued this is no more mysterious than other forms of interaction (e.g., forces between physical objects).
9.  **Evolutionary Theory of Language Functions:** He proposed an evolutionary hierarchy of language functions: from expressive, to signaling, to descriptive, and finally to the uniquely human **argumentative function**, which enables critical discussion and objective knowledge.
10. **The Self and its Plasticity:** The human self is not a fixed substance but a **theoretical abstraction**—a center of action, expectation, and critical reflection that is continually reshaped through its interaction with Worlds 1, 2, and especially 3.

### **Engagements with Other Thinkers (Part 2)**
1.  **Socrates vs. Plato:** He drew a sharp distinction. **Socrates** (the critical, dialogical seeker) was a hero of the open society, while **Plato** (the dogmatic system-builder who betrayed Socrates' critical spirit) was its first and most influential enemy.
2.  **Hegel as an Obfuscator:** He attacked Hegel not just for his historicism, but for his **"oracular" and obscure style**, which he saw as an intellectual betrayal of clarity and reason, paving the way for ideological manipulation.
3.  **Critique of Wittgenstein's "Language Games":** He saw Wittgenstein's later philosophy, with its emphasis on self-contained language games, as a form of **relativism** that could undermine the universal standards of rational criticism necessary for science and the open society.
4.  **Debate with Thomas Kuhn on "Normal Science":** He sharply criticized Kuhn's concept of "normal science" (puzzle-solving within a paradigm). Popper saw this as a period of **"dogmatic" thinking** and argued science is, or should be, in a state of "permanent revolution" fueled by criticism.
5.  **Dialectic as Uncritical: Hegel and Marx:** He rejected the Hegelian/Marxist dialectic as a **method of obscurantism** that avoids clear, testable statements. True progress comes from clear conjectures and refutations, not from a mysterious synthesis of contradictions.
6.  **Disagreement with the Frankfurt School:** While both were critical of positivism, Popper (in the "Positivism Dispute") accused thinkers like Adorno and Habermas of retreating into an **uncritical "hermeneutic" relativism** that abandoned the objective standards of logic and empirical testing.
7.  **Aristotle's Essentialism:** He linked Plato's political failings to **Aristotle's methodological essentialism** in science—the search for definitions and final causes—which he saw as a dead-end that hindered the development of a conjectural, problem-oriented science.
8.  **Engagement with Schrödinger on Realism:** He allied with fellow realist **Erwin Schrödinger** against the Copenhagen Interpretation's instrumentalism, arguing that the task of science is to propose bold, realistic conjectures about a world that exists independently of our observations.
9.  **The "Great Thinkers" as a Danger:** His project in *The Open Society* was to critique the **"oracular" intellectual authority** of Great Thinkers (Plato, Hegel, Marx), arguing that we must treat their ideas not as dogma but as hypotheses open to critical challenge.
10. **Common Ground with Hayek, with a Difference:** While both were anti-utopian and defenders of the open society, Popper believed more strongly in the potential of **reason and critical institutions** to shape social progress, whereas Hayek emphasized the limits of rational design and the wisdom of spontaneous order.

# Karl Popper's Contentions by Topic

## QUANTUM MECHANICS
1. The Copenhagen interpretation's claim that observation creates reality is false and represents an illegitimate intrusion of subjectivism into physics.
2. Probability in quantum mechanics should be understood in terms of objective propensities—real dispositional properties of physical systems—not as measures of subjective ignorance.
3. Quantum indeterminacy is an objective feature of reality, not merely a reflection of our epistemic limitations.
4. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle does not establish that microphysical reality is observer-dependent or that physics must abandon realism.
5. Quantum mechanics is fully compatible with scientific realism and does not require us to abandon the view that physics describes a mind-independent world.

## RELATIVITY THEORY
1. Einstein's general theory of relativity exemplifies genuine science because it makes precise, risky predictions that could in principle be falsified.
2. The Eddington eclipse expedition of 1919, which confirmed Einstein's prediction of light-bending, represents the ideal relationship between theory and empirical test.
3. Einstein's willingness to specify in advance what observations would refute his theory stands in stark contrast to the evasive strategies of pseudo-scientific theories.
4. Relativity theory demonstrates that revolutionary scientific progress occurs through bold conjectures that break radically with established frameworks.

## INDUCTION
1. There is no such thing as inductive inference; the supposed method of reasoning from particular observations to general laws is a myth.
2. The problem of induction, as formulated by Hume, is insoluble—but this is no loss, because science does not and need not rely on induction.
3. Scientific reasoning proceeds exclusively through conjecture and refutation: we propose bold hypotheses and then attempt to falsify them.
4. No finite number of confirming instances can ever establish the truth or even the probability of a universal generalization.
5. The psychological feeling that repeated observations justify generalizations is merely habit, not rational inference.
6. All human knowledge is conjectural and fallible; we never achieve certainty, only progressively better approximations to truth.

## CAUSATION
1. Causal explanation remains legitimate in science, but determinism—the thesis that all events are uniquely necessitated by prior causes—is false.
2. The universe is genuinely indeterministic, containing objective propensities rather than fixed causal chains.
3. Propensities are real dispositional properties of situations that generate objective probabilities for various outcomes.
4. Causal laws in science should be understood as describing tendencies or propensities rather than exceptionless necessities.

## THE MIND-BODY PROBLEM
1. The mind and the brain are distinct but causally interacting realities; interactionist dualism is the correct view.
2. Reality comprises three ontologically distinct worlds: World 1 (physical objects and states), World 2 (mental states and subjective experiences), and World 3 (objective knowledge and abstract contents).
3. Materialism and physicalism are false because they cannot account for the reality of consciousness and subjective experience.
4. Epiphenomenalism is false because mental states have genuine causal efficacy in producing physical effects.
5. The self is real and unified, not an illusion generated by brain processes.
6. World 2 (the mental) serves as an intermediary through which World 3 (objective knowledge) can affect World 1 (the physical).

## PLATONIC ENTITIES / WORLD 3
1. Abstract objects—theories, problems, arguments, mathematical entities—exist objectively and constitute a distinct ontological realm (World 3).
2. World 3 objects are human creations, but once created they become autonomous and contain unintended consequences that can be discovered.
3. The content of a theory is distinct from any physical embodiment or psychological act of thinking; it has objective existence.
4. Mathematical objects and logical relations exist independently of whether anyone thinks about them.
5. Problems can exist objectively even when no one is aware of them; they await discovery.

## OBJECTIVE KNOWLEDGE
1. Knowledge can exist without a knowing subject; it exists objectively in World 3 as the content of theories, books, and arguments.
2. The traditional definition of knowledge as justified true belief is mistaken; all knowledge is conjectural and revisable.
3. Objective knowledge grows through the elimination of error, not through the accumulation of justified beliefs.
4. A theory can constitute objective knowledge even if no one currently believes it or understands it.
5. The growth of knowledge is the most important feature of human existence and the engine of all progress.

## THE ASYMMETRY BETWEEN PREDICTION AND RETRODICTION
1. There is a fundamental logical asymmetry between predicting the future and explaining the past.
2. We can often explain past events that we could not have predicted, because we can identify the relevant initial conditions only after the fact.
3. The impossibility of predicting historical events does not entail the impossibility of explaining them retrospectively.
4. The past is fixed and can be investigated; the future is genuinely open and cannot be known in advance.

## THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN EXPLANATION AND PREDICTION
1. Genuine scientific explanation and prediction are logically symmetrical in structure: both involve deducing particular events from general laws plus initial conditions.
2. However, the symmetry thesis must be qualified: some explanations (especially in historical and social sciences) do not yield corresponding predictions.
3. The ability to explain an event after it has occurred does not guarantee the ability to have predicted it.
4. Predictive success is a crucial test of a theory's explanatory adequacy, but lack of predictive power does not automatically disqualify an explanation.

## THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN EXPLANATION AND RETRODICTION
1. Retrodictive explanation—explaining past events—is legitimate even when corresponding predictions would have been impossible.
2. In evolutionary biology and historical sciences, we can explain outcomes by identifying selective pressures and historical circumstances, even though we could not have predicted those outcomes.
3. The covering-law model of explanation applies to retrodiction: we explain past events by showing how they follow from laws and initial conditions.

## VIEWS ON PRAGMATISM AND INSTRUMENTALISM
1. Instrumentalism about scientific theories is false; theories are not merely useful tools for prediction but genuine attempts to describe reality.
2. The pragmatist equation of truth with usefulness is mistaken; a theory can be useful yet false, and true yet currently useless.
3. Science aims at truth, not merely at practical success or predictive utility.
4. To treat theories as mere instruments is to misunderstand the nature of scientific inquiry and to abandon the pursuit of understanding.

## VIEWS ON LOGICAL POSITIVISM
1. The verification principle of meaning is self-refuting and must be rejected; it is neither analytic nor empirically verifiable.
2. The positivist criterion of demarcation (meaningfulness through verifiability) should be replaced by falsifiability as the criterion distinguishing science from non-science.
3. The sharp distinction between observational and theoretical terms is untenable; all observation is theory-laden.
4. Metaphysics is not meaningless, even if it is not empirically testable; many scientific theories began as metaphysical speculation.
5. The positivist program of reducing all knowledge to sensory experience fails; science requires bold theoretical conjectures that transcend observation.

## THE ONTOLOGICAL STATUS OF THEORETICAL ENTITIES
1. Theoretical entities such as electrons, fields, and genes are not mere logical fictions or computational devices; they are real existents.
2. Scientific realism is correct: our best theories provide approximately true descriptions of unobservable entities and processes.
3. The success of science in prediction and technological application is best explained by the assumption that our theories are at least approximately true.
4. To be an instrumentalist about theoretical entities is to abandon the goal of understanding and to render the success of science miraculous.

## HISTORICISM
1. Historicism—the doctrine that history is governed by discoverable laws enabling prediction of its future course—is fundamentally mistaken.
2. The future of human history cannot be predicted by any scientific method, because the growth of human knowledge is inherently unpredictable.
3. There are no laws of historical development analogous to the laws of physics; history is shaped by unique, unrepeatable events.
4. Belief in historical destiny is not only false but dangerous, as it encourages fatalism and justifies totalitarian social engineering.
5. Piecemeal social engineering, not utopian planning based on supposed historical laws, is the only rational approach to social reform.

## MARXISM
1. Marxism is a pseudo-science because it is unfalsifiable; its adherents immunize it against refutation by ad hoc modifications.
2. Marx's specific predictions—such as the immiseration of the proletariat and revolution in advanced capitalist countries—have been empirically refuted.
3. The Marxist response to failed predictions—reinterpreting the theory to accommodate contrary evidence—demonstrates its unscientific character.
4. Marxism's claim to scientific status masked an essentially religious or dogmatic commitment impervious to criticism.
5. Marxist historicism, with its doctrine of inevitable historical progress toward communism, is both false and politically pernicious.
6. The totalitarian regimes inspired by Marxism represent the practical consequences of historicist and utopian thinking.

## HEGELIANISM
1. Hegel's philosophy is largely unintelligible bombast that has exercised a catastrophic influence on intellectual and political history.
2. The Hegelian dialectic is not a valid logical method but a rhetorical device for evading criticism and contradiction.
3. Hegel's identification of the real with the rational served to justify the Prussian state and laid the groundwork for totalitarian ideology.
4. Hegel's historicism—his doctrine that history is the unfolding of Absolute Spirit toward a predetermined goal—is both false and dangerous.
5. Hegel bears significant intellectual responsibility for the totalitarian movements of the twentieth century, having provided the philosophical framework for both fascism and Marxism.

## FREUD / FREUDIANISM
1. Psychoanalysis is a pseudo-science because no conceivable observation could ever refute it; any behavior can be "explained" by Freudian theory.
2. The apparent explanatory power of psychoanalysis is illusory; its ability to accommodate all possible evidence demonstrates not its truth but its emptiness.
3. Freud's theories fail the criterion of falsifiability that distinguishes genuine science from pseudo-science.
4. The contrast between Freud's unfalsifiable claims and Einstein's risky, testable predictions illustrates the difference between pseudo-science and genuine science.
5. Psychoanalytic "confirmations" through clinical observation are worthless, because the analyst's theoretical commitments shape what is observed.
6. The mythology of the unconscious, with its alleged mechanisms of repression and wish-fulfillment, lacks empirical warrant.
