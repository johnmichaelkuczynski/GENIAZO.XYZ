### **1.0 What is ethics?**
1. **There are two kinds of statements: normative and descriptive.**  
   > “There are two kinds of statements: normative and descriptive.”
2. **Descriptive statements do not express value-judgments.**  
   > “Descriptive statements don’t express value-judgments.”
3. **Normative statements express value-judgments.**  
   > “A normative statement is one that does express a value-judgment.”
4. **Ethics is the discipline that attempts to clarify the structure of normative concepts.**  
   > “Ethics is the discipline that attempts to clarify the structures of these concepts.”

---

### **2.0 Two kinds of goodness: instrumental and intrinsic**
5. **The term “good” is ambiguous.**  
   > “For each of these terms is ambiguous.”
6. **Instrumental goodness refers to acts that benefit the agent.**  
   > “To say that an act is instrumentally good is to say that it has consequences that are desired by, or to the advantage of, the agent.”
7. **Ethics is concerned with intrinsic, not instrumental, goodness.**  
   > “Ethics is concerned with intrinsic goodness.”
8. **Ethics asks: “Which things are intrinsically good?”**  
   > “Ethics attempts to answer the question ‘which things are intrinsically good?’”
9. **Happiness, intelligence, benevolence, honesty, etc., are examples of intrinsic goods.**  
   > “happiness, intelligence, benevolence, honesty… are intrinsically good.”
10. **There are different kinds of freedom: physical, emotional, intellectual.**  
    > “there is freedom from external, physical coercion… emotional freedom… intellectual freedom.”

---

### **2.1 Instrumental and intrinsic goodness are not mutually exclusive**
11. **Some things are both instrumentally and intrinsically good.**  
    > “Having a sharp intellect is both an intrinsic good and an instrumental good.”
12. **Happiness, friendship, and honesty can be both instrumental and intrinsic goods.**  
    > “What we just said about intelligence is true of happiness, of pleasure, of friendship, and of honesty.”

### **3.0 More meanings of “good”: goodness vs. commendableness**
13. **The expression “intrinsic good” is itself ambiguous.**  
    > “the expression ‘intrinsic good’ is itself ambiguous.”
14. **Some intrinsic goods are praiseworthy; others are not.**  
    > “Some things that are intrinsically good are commendable—that is, they deserve praise. And some things that are intrinsically good do not deserve praise.”
15. **Happiness is intrinsically good but not necessarily praiseworthy.**  
    > “happiness is non-instrumentally good, while at the same time not being commendable.”
16. **Some intrinsic goods (like charitable acts) are praiseworthy.**  
    > “Jones’ behavior is obviously praiseworthy—that is, it is commendable, and it is also intrinsically good.”
17. **Natural talent is intrinsically good but not praiseworthy.**  
    > “Smith’s musical talent is intrinsically good… But Smith doesn’t deserve any praise for happening to have been blessed with this talent.”
18. **There are two kinds of intrinsic goods: commendable and non-commendable.**  
    > “there are two kinds of intrinsic goods—those which are commendable and those which are not.”

### **3.1 Two kinds of intrinsic badness**
19. **There are two kinds of intrinsic badness: condemnable and non-condemnable.**  
    > “Just as there are two kinds of intrinsic goodness, so there are two kinds of intrinsic badness.”
20. **Unhappiness is intrinsically bad but not always condemnable.**  
    > “It’s obviously bad to be unhappy. And the badness in question is clearly of a non-instrumental, and therefore intrinsic, nature.”
21. **Causing unnecessary suffering is both intrinsically bad and condemnable.**  
    > “Causing somebody else to suffer, for no good reason, is intrinsically bad and it is condemnable.”
22. **“Intrinsically bad” is not always identical with “worthy of condemnation.”**  
    > “‘intrinsically bad’ is not, at least not always, identical with ‘worthy of condemnation.’”

### **4.0 The morally complex structure of some situations**
23. **A single act can be both intrinsically good and intrinsically bad in different respects.**  
    > “A single act or state of affairs can be intrinsically good in one respect and intrinsically bad in some other respect.”
24. **An act can be both condemnable and commendable.**  
    > “A given act can be both condemnable and commendable.”

### **5.0 The non-privative character of moral attributes**
25. **Moral properties are not privative; they are positive properties.**  
    > “Neither property is ‘privative’; they are both ‘positive’ properties.”
26. **Badness is not merely the absence of goodness.**  
    > “badness, like goodness, is the presence of something.”
27. **People mistakenly think good and bad cannot coexist because they see them as opposites.**  
    > “people tend to think that good and bad cannot co-exist in the same person, or the same act, because they wrongly think that good is the absence of bad and that badness is the absence of good.”
28. **People think in binary terms about morality due to cognitive ease.**  
    > “People tend, especially where moral and emotional issues are concerned, to think in an unrealistically binary way.”

---

### **6.0 Moral obligations have a dimension of weight**
29. **Moral obligations are not binary; they come in degrees.**  
    > “Moral obligations are not ‘binary’; they come in degrees.”
30. **Some obligations are stronger than others.**  
    > “some obligations are therefore stronger than others.”
31. **There are degrees of wrongness.**  
    > “A related point is that there are degrees of wrongness.”

---

### **6.1 For one moral obligation to outweigh another is not for it to cancel it**
32. **Conflicting obligations can coexist.**  
    > “Oftentimes, we have conflicting obligations.”
33. **An overridden obligation still exists.**  
    > “obligations that are outweighed by other, more powerful obligations do not on that account cease to exist.”

---

### **6.2 “right” ≠ “good”; “right” = “least bad”**
34. **The right action is the least bad option available.**  
    > “what we mean by ‘the right course of action’ is the least bad course of action that the circumstances permit.”

---

### **6.3 “wrong” ≠ “bad”; “wrong” = “least good”**
35. **A wrong action is not necessarily a bad action; it is the least good possible option.**  
    > “For a course of action to be wrong is for it to be the least good possible course of action.”

---

### **7.0 “Ought” implies “can”**
36. **One has an obligation only if one can fulfill it.**  
    > “It is a generally accepted principle of ethics that one has an obligation to do something only if one can do it.”
37. **The principle must be qualified in cases of conflicting obligations.**  
    > “We saw that there are circumstances where one has multiple conflicting obligations… where it isn’t strictly correct to say that ‘ought’ implies ‘can.’”

---

### **8.0 Legality ≠ morality**
38. **Legal acts can be immoral.**  
    > “There are, or at least can be, acts which are legal, even legally required, which are immoral.”
39. **Legality and morality sometimes overlap.**  
    > “At the same time, legality and morality often overlap.”

### **9.0 The moral status of passing judgment on X ≠ the moral status of X itself**
40. **Knowing a fact does not entitle one to assert it without evidence.**  
    > “Given only that some object in fact weighs 200 pounds, I don’t necessarily know, or therefore have the right to say, that it has that weight.”
41. **It can be wrong to condemn what is in fact condemnable if one lacks sufficient evidence.**  
    > “it can be condemnable to condemn the condemnable.”

### **10.0 The goodness of the act vs. the goodness of the agent**
42. **Praise depends on intention, not outcome.**  
    > “Whether somebody deserves to be commended for an act depends, not on whether it actually was the right act, but on whether it was the agent’s intention to perform the right act.”
43. **Two agents with identical intentions deserve the same moral evaluation, regardless of outcome.**  
    > “Mary’s intentions are exactly like Kathy’s… they are equally worthy of praise.”
44. **An agent who intends evil but brings about good still deserves condemnation.**  
    > “Betty clearly deserves condemnation of the most severe kind… because, even though she ended up doing good, it was not her intention to do so.”

### **11.0 Why attempted crimes are punished less severely**
45. **Failure to commit a crime may indicate lack of resolve or moral reservation.**

46. **The legal system’s approach is consistent with the intention-based view of blame.**

### **12.0 Condemnation of Hitler despite possible “good intentions”**

68. **Factual statements can also be disputed without being mere opinion.**

### **15.2 Ethical positions can be rationally disputed**
70. **Ethical debates often turn on factual questions.**

### **15.3 There is considerable ethical agreement**
71. **Many ethical questions have clear, agreed-upon answers.**  
    > “Is it morally okay to torture babies for the sheer fun of it? No.”
72. **The illusion of disagreement arises because settled issues are no longer debated.**  
    > “The illusion that there is no moral agreement… is that an ethical issue isn’t worth discussing anymore if there is universal agreement about it.”

### **15.4 Ethical disagreement often stems from unresolved factual questions**
73. **Debates over the death penalty depend on factual questions (e.g., execution of innocents).**  
    > “The question ‘how many innocent people are executed for crimes they didn’t commit?’ is a strictly factual question.”

### **16.0 What are ethical truths?**
75. **Ethical assertions may be disguised factual assertions.**  
    > “ethical assertions are factual assertions, albeit disguised ones.”
76. **Utilitarianism holds that good acts are those that maximize happiness.**  
    > “According to one version of it, which is known as ‘utilitarianism,’ good things are those that bring about happiness.”
77. **Another view holds that good acts promote human flourishing.**  
    > “for something to be good is for it to be identical with, or logically required by, a practice on which human flourishing depends.”
78. **If these views are correct, moral questions are factual questions.**  
    > “if the just-described moral view is correct, moral questions are factual questions.”
